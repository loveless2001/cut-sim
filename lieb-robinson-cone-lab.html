The Lieb–Robinson Cone EST

Any quantum system with local interactions has a maximum speed at which correlations can spread — the Lieb–Robinson bound, a proven theorem. This is the framework's candidate for component C1 of Lorentz invariance: an emergent, finite, pre-geometric speed of light.

But here is the obstruction GAP: on a structured substrate that speed is generically direction-dependent. Why this matters to the framework

The cone is real, finite — and lumpy. A finite maximum correlation speed comes for free from local interactions (C1 ✓). But Lorentz invariance needs more: a perfectly round cone, identical for every observer. What you see instead is an ellipse with a preferred rest frame — "Galilean with a speed limit," not Lorentzian. The ratio Agod = vx/vy = Jx/Jy quantifies the lumpiness that any emergence story must somehow hide.

The measurement smuggles in a ruler. Calling this cone elliptical uses site-counting and the global clock — a "view from nowhere." The framework's question is whether an observer built out of this same lattice, with rods and clocks made of the same couplings, could ever measure that ellipticity. That test — with its measured verdict — is the Verdict Lab.
